I
When golden rays do touch the morning’s brow,
And wake the sleeping lilies from the field,
To Time’s sharp scythe the stoutest oak must bow,
And all the summer’s pride to winter yield.
But in thy face a second spring I find,
Where roses bloom despite the biting frost,
A treasure kept within a quiet mind,
To stay the heavy toll of beauty lost.
Let not the glass thy changing features chide,
Nor tell the hours that steal away thy youth,
For in my verse thy glory shall abide,
The living witness of thy constant truth.
So long as men have eyes to see this grace,
Thy light shall shine from out this hallowed place.

II
Observe the glass where silvered lines do creep,
Like silent thieves across a marble hall,
For while the weary world is hushed in sleep,
The sands of age within the hourglass fall.
Thy youth is but a lease of fleeting date,
A borrowed sum that Nature must reclaim,
Unless thou do defy the hand of Fate,
And give a child the shadow of thy name.
Then should the cold and winter of thy days
Be warmed by embers of a younger fire,
And thou shalt hear again the notes of praise,
Within the music of a new desire.
To live but once is Fortune’s cruelest jest,
To live in heirs is how the soul is blest.

III
As mariners do seek the northern star,
To guide their heavy hulls through tossing foam,
So do I watch thy spirit from afar,
To lead my wandering heart to safety home.
The clouds may veil the brightness of thy gaze,
And tempests shake the pillars of my trust,
Yet through the dark and labyrinthine maze,
Thy love remains when all is turned to dust.
No compass needle finds a truer path,
Than that which points my longing unto thee,
Though I should bide the ocean’s salty wrath,
Or drift upon a deep and silent sea.
For thou art fixed while all the world does turn,
The sacred lamp whereat my soul does burn.

IV
What usury is this, that thou shouldst owe
The very breath that makes thy pulses beat?
For Nature gave thee seeds that thou mightst sow,
And find a harvest in the summer heat.
But if thou hoard the riches of thy frame,
And keep thy beauty locked within a chest,
Thou dost but bring a shadow to thy name,
And leave the world of all thy wealth bereft.
The miser dies with gold he cannot spend,
While he who gives does find his store increase,
Be not thy beauty’s foe, but be its friend,
And grant the coming years a fair release.
Thy self-love is a debt thou canst not pay,
Unless thou give thy loveliness away.

V
When I behold the mountain’s purple crest,
Begin to fade beneath the evening’s grey,
I think how all things hasten to their rest,
And follow out the footsteps of the day.
The busy lark forgets her morning song,
The weary plowman leaves the heavy soil,
For night is short and hidden paths are long,
And sleep is all the recompense for toil.
Yet in the dark thy image doth appear,
A radiant sun that mocks the gloomy night,
To banish every shadow, every fear,
And fill my heavy soul with sudden light.
Though day be fled and all the world be dim,
My heart doth sing its secret morning hymn.

VI
Let not the monuments of ancient kings,
Boast of a life that triumphs over death,
For Time shall tear away their stone-built wings,
And choke their praises with a dusty breath.
The gilded dome shall crumble to the ground,
The brass shall tarnish in the salty air,
And where a mighty city once was found,
The weeds shall grow and make their dwellings there.
But this my verse, a temple built of rhyme,
Shall stand against the battery of the years,
A fortress in the shifting sands of Time,
To keep thy name from out the vale of tears.
While ink can flow and men have breath to read,
Thy life shall blossom in this little seed.

VII
If I should count the virtues of thy heart,
And write them down in rows of black and white,
The world would say I played a painter’s part,
And drew a ghost to mock the living light.
They would not credit half the grace I see,
Nor trust the words that labor to be true,
For heaven’s self is mirrored deep in thee,
In colors of a rare and heavenly hue.
So I must hoard my praise within my breast,
And speak in riddles to the passing crowd,
Lest thy perfection be a common guest,
And wrapped within a common, dusty shroud.
My silent love is truer than my tongue,
Which leaves the best of thee as yet unsung.

VIII
Thy love is like the alchemy of old,
That turns the leaden sorrows of my day,
Into a store of bright and burnished gold,
And drives the heavy mists of grief away.
When Fortune frowns and all my friends depart,
And I am left to dwell in low estate,
I find a kingdom hidden in thy heart,
That makes me mock the iron hand of Fate.
No king can boast a crown so rich as mine,
Nor sultan rule a land so fair to see,
For all the wealth of India’s deepest mine,
Is nothing to the love thou bearest me.
Let others seek the world for tawny ore,
I find in thee an inexhausted store.

IX
The rose does wear a crown of cruel thorn,
To guard the velvet petals of her pride,
Yet by the wind her fragrant soul is torn,
And in the dust her fallen glories hide.
So does the world deal with a lovely face,
And prick the heart that dares to bloom so fair,
Till Time has robbed it of its youthful grace,
And left but thorns of sorrow and despair.
But thou hast found a garden in my mind,
Where no sharp winter wind shall ever blow,
A sheltered spot where thou shalt surely find,
The strength to let thy inner beauty grow.
The rose may die when summer’s lease is run,
But thou shalt flourish in a lasting sun.

X
When in the chronicles of wasted time,
I see the brave and beautiful portrayed,
In antique verse and many a flowing rhyme,
Before the debt of mortal life was paid,
I see that all their praises were but dreams,
Of what thy living self does now possess,
They were but candles, catching distant gleams,
Of thy unshadowed, peerless loveliness.
For thou art all the beauty they did feign,
The very substance of their airy thought,
The cure for every ancient poet’s pain,
The holy prize that every singer sought.
They looked through glass and saw a dim surmise,
But I behold the truth within thine eyes.

XI
Is it a crime to love what must decay,
To cast my heart upon a shifting sand?
For even now the sun begins to stray,
Towards the edges of the western land.
The leaf is green before it turns to gold,
The bud is tight before it learns to fly,
And everything that’s young must soon be old,
And everything that’s born is born to die.
Yet in this change there is a constant soul,
A thread of fire that runs through every vein,
That makes the broken parts a perfect whole,
And brings the summer back to us again.
I love thee not for what the years can steal,
But for the eternal truth thy looks reveal.

XII
Not from the stars do I my judgment pluck,
Nor do I read the secrets of the moon,
To tell if thou shalt have a run of luck,
Or if thy sun shall set at highest noon.
I seek no magic in the dregs of wine,
Nor watch the flight of birds across the sky,
To see if Fortune’s favor shall be thine,
Or if thy hopes are destined soon to die.
But in thine eyes I read my only fate,
There is the heaven where my soul would dwell,
There is the book that tells of love or hate,
My only paradise, my only hell.
The stars may wander in their paths above,
I only follow where I find thy love.

XIII
Weary with toil, I haste me to my bed,
The dear repose for limbs with travel tired,
But then begins a journey in my head,
To seek the grace that I have long desired.
For then my thoughts, from far away and near,
Do make a pilgrimage unto thy sight,
And keep my drooping eyelids open wide,
To watch thy shadow through the heavy night.
Like to a jeweler who works by stealth,
To set a pearl within a darksome frame,
My fancy takes the treasure of thy wealth,
And whispers to the dark thy holy name.
By day my limbs do find their heavy task,
By night my soul does in thy presence bask.

XIV
The lawyer pleads with subtle, silver tongue,
To prove the right where only wrong is found,
The ancient bell by heavy hands is rung,
To shake the air with deep and hollow sound.
But love requires no witness for its cause,
Nor seeks a jury in the crowded street,
It stands above the reach of human laws,
And makes the bitter taste of sorrow sweet.
I will not sue for favor at thy hand,
Nor bring a scroll of merits to thy door,
For love is like the tide upon the sand,
That gives its all and then returns for more.
No judge can sentence me to leave thy side,
If in thy heart I may at peace abide.

XV
My thoughts are jurors in a silent court,
Wherein thy beauty stands as evidence,
And though my heart would make a false report,
Thy virtue is its own surest defense.
The eyes may swear they saw a fleeting grace,
The ears may claim they heard a silver tone,
But truth is written only in thy face,
A testimony thou dost hold alone.
No advocate can plead against thy light,
No witness bring a shadow to thy name,
For in the very middle of the night,
Thy radiance puts the midday sun to shame.
Let all the world its heavy judgment bring,
I find my justice in the song I sing.

XVI
When clouds of doubt do overcast my sky,
And hide the warmth of thy accustomed smile,
I feel the heavy hours go dragging by,
And count each minute as a weary mile.
The world becomes a cold and barren place,
Where no bird sings and no green leaf is seen,
For I have lost the vision of thy face,
The only sun that keeps my spirit green.
But then a word, a look, a sudden thought,
Does break the clouds and let the light shine through,
And all the peace that I have vainly sought,
Is given back by one small glimpse of you.
So does the storm-tossed sailor find the shore,
And vow to tempt the angry seas no more.

XVII
Say that thou didst forsake me for some fault,
And I will comment then upon my sin,
For I will join with thee in thy assault,
And prove that I am black and foul within.
Thou canst not vex me with a sharper sting,
Than to agree that I am base and low,
For from thy judgment no appeal I bring,
But bow my head and let the sentence go.
Yet even in my shame I find a pride,
That thou shouldst be so holy and so pure,
That when thou turnest from my side,
Thy very coldness makes my love more sure.
For if thou hatest what is vile in me,
I love thee more for thy integrity.

XVIII
What is the soul but like a master lute,
That waits the touch of some inspired hand?
For else its strings are motionless and mute,
Like silent forests in a lonely land.
Thou art the player who does wake the strain,
And strike the chords of passion and of peace,
To turn to music every throb of pain,
And grant the heavy heart a sweet release.
The melody that rises from the wood,
Is not the wood’s, but comes from thy command,
For everything of beautiful and good,
Is but the work of thy transforming hand.
I am the instrument, and thou the song,
To whom the echoes of my life belong.

XIX
If every shadow were a mourning veil,
To shroud the brightness of a summer’s day,
The cheek of morning would turn thin and pale,
And all the joy of light would fade away.
But in the darkness of thy raven hair,
I find a luster that the sun lacks power,
A midnight beauty, exquisitely rare,
That blooms within the quiet, secret hour.
Let others praise the lily and the rose,
And seek the colors that the morning brings,
My heart a deeper, darker mystery knows,
And of a twilight glory softly sings.
For in thy gloom there is a holy spark,
That guides my spirit through the heavy dark.

XX
The curtain falls upon the final act,
The lights grow dim within the empty hall,
And what was once a grand and living fact,
Is now but shadows flickering on the wall.
The actors lay their painted masks aside,
And step from out the roles they briefly bore,
For nothing in this pageant can abide,
When Death stands knocking at the theater door.
But though the stage be dark and silence reign,
The poet’s word outlives the player’s breath,
To bring the story back to life again,
And snatch a glory from the jaws of Death.
So shall these lines, when I am turned to clay,
Keep thy bright spirit in the light of day.

XXI
When golden dawn doth paint the eastern sky,
And wakes the lark within the dewy field,
I see the glory of thy youthful eye,
To which the sun’s own radiance must yield.
But as the day to evening shadows flows,
And summer’s prime is stolen by the frost,
So shall the bloom that on thy temple grows
In winter’s heavy count be surely lost.
Yet fear thou not the scythe of hungry Time,
Nor all the spoils that age may seek to take,
For in the music of this humble rhyme,
Thy living grace a second life shall make.
Though marble tombs may crumble into dust,
In lines of verse, thy beauty holds its trust.

XXII
Behold the oak that braved the winter’s blast,
With branches bared against the frozen sky;
Its leafy crown is numbered with the past,
And all its emerald pride is forced to die.
So too shall strength within thy pulses fail,
And silver threads among thy tresses creep,
When all thy vigor proves of no avail,
And thou art summoned to the final sleep.
But let not sorrow dim thy sparkling sight,
Nor heavy sighs consume thy gentle breast,
For love shall kindle an eternal light,
And grant thy weary spirit quiet rest.
Death hath no sting where true devotion dwells,
As every note of this soft measure tells.

XXIII
As some unskilful pilot on the sea,
Who steers his bark without a guiding star,
So do I wander when I part from thee,
And drift upon the foaming waves afar.
The compass of my heart is turned to thine,
Thy love the port where I would find my peace,
Yet tempest-tossed in bitter shade I pine,
Until the raging of the waters cease.
O, let thy favor be the steady light
That leads me safely through the surging tide,
To banish all the terrors of the night,
And keep me ever constant at thy side.
For without thee, my soul is cast away,
A lonely wreck beneath the salt-sea spray.

XXIV
The busy gardener with his pruning knife
Doth lop the wayward branches from the vine,
To grant the swelling fruit a fuller life,
And make the vineyard’s heavy harvest shine.
So Time, the mower with his cruel blade,
Doth trim away the blossoms of thy youth,
Until thy fairness is a ghostly shade,
And thou art left with nothing but the truth.
But I shall plant thy virtues in my mind,
And water them with tears of pure delight,
That in my soul a garden thou shalt find,
Forever green and sheltered from the blight.
No winter wind shall strip thy petals bare,
While thou art tended with such loving care.

XXV
The miser counts his gold with greedy hand,
And locks his treasures in a heavy chest,
Yet cannot buy a grain of shifting sand,
To stay the sun from sinking in the west.
Thy beauty is a wealth more precious far
Than all the riches of the Indian shore,
Yet even this the hand of age shall mar,
And leave thee poorer than thou wert before.
But I have stored thy image in my heart,
A secret hoard that none can steal away,
To keep thee whole when all thy charms depart,
And darkness falls upon thy closing day.
Thy worth shall grow as worldly goods decline,
For all I have is doubled, being thine.

XXVI
What legal bond can tie a soul to soul,
Or what indenture seal a lover’s vow?
No parchment scroll can ever grant control,
Or place a crown upon a lover’s brow.
For love is free as is the wandering air,
That blows where’er it lists across the lea,
And mocks the heavy chains of dark despair,
To set the captive spirit truly free.
Yet I am bound to thee by silver threads,
More strong than iron or the weight of brass,
While others follow where their fancy leads,
And watch the fleeting moments as they pass.
My heart is captive to thy gentle will,
And in that bondage finds its freedom still.

XXVII
When midnight bells do toll the hour of rest,
And all the world is wrapped in silent sleep,
I lie awake with heavy cares oppressed,
And lonely vigils in the darkness keep.
Thy phantom form appears before my sight,
To haunt the weary chambers of my brain,
With eyes that shine like jewels in the night,
To ease the burden of my secret pain.
But when the sun dispels the morning mist,
And calls the world to labor and to strife,
I find the lips that I in fancy kissed
Are vanished from the portals of my life.
O, let the day be brief and night be long,
That I may hear again thy silent song.

XXVIII
The weary sun descends into the deep,
To hide his face behind the western hill,
And leaves the world in sullen gloom to weep,
While every bird and beast is hushed and still.
But in my heart a brighter sun doth rise,
To banish all the shadows of the night,
Reflected from the heavens of thy eyes,
To fill my spirit with a holy light.
No darkness can extinguish such a flame,
Nor all the clouds of sorrow dim its glow,
For I shall whisper thy beloved name,
And find a solace that the world can't know.
Though night may reign across the lonely land,
I walk in brightness, guided by thy hand.

XXIX
The painter seeks with brush and varied hue
To catch the fleeting shadows on the wall,
To give the world a vision ever new,
Before the evening's dusky curtains fall.
But how can colors on a canvas spread
Portray the living fire within thy soul,
Or mimic all the graces thou hast shed,
To make my broken spirit once more whole?
The artist’s art is but a feeble thing,
A mimicry of what is truly fair,
For none can paint the music that you sing,
Or trap thy essence in a worldly snare.
Thy beauty lives within my verse alone,
In lines more lasting than the carven stone.

XXX
When I sit silent in the court of thought,
And summon up the ghosts of buried years,
I see the battles that I vainly fought,
And drown my spirit in a sea of tears.
For every joy has paid a heavy toll,
And every hope has withered on the stem,
While sorrow takes a tribute from my soul,
To deck its brow with many a bitter gem.
But if I chance to think on thee, my friend,
The clouds of grief are scattered by the wind,
My heavy labors find a happy end,
And all my losses are at once resigned.
Thy love is recompense for every woe,
The greatest gift that heaven can bestow.

XXXI
The alchemist within his smoky cell
Doth seek to turn the base to purest gold,
With secret charms and many a magic spell,
To find the riches that the earth doth hold.
But I have found a greater craft than his,
To change the dross of life to something rare,
For in the rapture of a lover’s kiss,
I banish all the weight of dull despair.
Thy touch is magic to my weary heart,
Thy voice a balm for every hidden wound,
And by the power of thy wondrous art,
My spirit is with heavenly glory crowned.
Let others search for stones of mystic might,
I find my treasure in thy holy light.

XXXII
The soldier weary from the bloody field,
Who hangs his battered armor on the wall,
Doth find a solace that the wars can’t yield,
To wait in silence for the evening call.
So I, who long have fought the wars of love,
And borne the scars of many a sharp disdain,
Do look for comfort from the stars above,
To ease the throb of my long-suffered pain.
Yet all my wounds are healed by thy regard,
And all my battles are at last forgot,
For in thy grace I find my great reward,
Whatever be the nature of my lot.
The peace I find within thy gentle arms
Is proof against the world’s most dire alarms.

XXXIII
The falconer doth watch the soaring hawk,
That circles high above the tufted grass,
And hears the echoes of its lonely talk,
As through the silver clouds it seeks to pass.
But though it flies on wings of wild desire,
It must return at last to its master’s hand,
To quench the burning of its inner fire,
And find its rest within its native land.
So does my spirit wander far and wide,
To seek the wonders of the earth and sea,
Yet ever turns as with a rising tide,
To find its only home at last in thee.
I am the hawk and thou the steady lure,
That keeps my roving heart forever pure.

XXXIV
The moon is hidden by a dark eclipse,
And all the stars are muffled in a shroud,
While silence seals the music of thy lips,
And wraps our passion in a heavy cloud.
A sudden quarrel like a winter storm
Has chilled the warmth of our accustomed grace,
And left my spirit lacking all its form,
To wander in a dark and lonely place.
But let the sun of thy forgiveness shine,
To melt the ice that gathers in my breast,
And let thy hand be folded once in mine,
To give my troubled heart a moment’s rest.
For though the heavens may be dark a while,
My world is lighted only by thy smile.

XXXV
The changing seasons in their steady pace
Do bring the blossom and the falling leaf,
And leave their mark on every mortal face,
In lines of joy and characters of grief.
But what is written with a trembling pen
May outlast all the empires of the earth,
And speak to generations of young men
Of all thy beauty and thy matchless worth.
Though time may strip the forest of its green,
And turn the summer to a barren waste,
Thy living image shall be clearly seen,
By those who read these lines with eager haste.
For ink is stronger than the hand of fate,
To keep thy glory in its pristine state.

XXXVI
The pilgrim travels to a distant shrine,
Across the desert and the mountain height,
To seek a vision of the grace divine,
And find a comfort in the holy light.
My journey leads me to thy lovely face,
The sacred altar where I bow my head,
To find a refuge in thy quiet grace,
And follow where thy gentle feet have led.
No weary mile can dampen my desire,
No rugged path can turn my steps away,
For in my soul there burns a holy fire,
That guides me safely through the darkest day.
Thou art the temple where my heart doth dwell,
More fair than any tongue can ever tell.

XXXVII
The lutanist with light and nimble hand
Doth strike the strings to make a joyous sound,
That echoes far across the summer land,
And spreads a magic through the air around.
But when thy voice is heard within the room,
All other music seems a discord harsh,
Like winter thunder in the gathering gloom,
Or lonely winds across a frozen marsh.
For thou art tuned to such a perfect pitch,
That every word is like a silver bell,
To make the poorest beggar truly rich,
And cast o’er all the world a holy spell.
My heart is but a lute for thee to play,
To drive the shadows of the world away.

XXXVIII
The architect with plumb and leveling line
Doth build a monument of heavy stone,
To make his name with lasting glory shine,
When all his mortal days are past and gone.
But I do build a tower of simple words,
To house the beauty that is truly thine,
More light and airy than the song of birds,
Yet more enduring than the mountain pine.
For stone will crumble and the brass decay,
And all the works of man will fall to dust,
But these my lines shall never pass away,
To keep thy image as a sacred trust.
A monument I raise within this verse,
Thy living praise for ever to rehearse.

XXXIX
The shadow follows where the body goes,
Across the meadow in the light of noon,
Or where the quiet river softly flows,
Beneath the silver circles of the moon.
So does my love attend thy every move,
A silent witness to thy daily grace,
To seek a sign that thou dost well approve,
And find my heaven in thy lovely face.
But when the sun is hidden from the sight,
The shadow vanishes into the air,
And leaves me in the stillness of the night,
To wrestle with the demons of despair.
O, be my sun and let me be thy shade,
That of our union may a world be made.

XL
I look within the mirror of the years,
And see the changes that the time hath wrought,
The traces of a thousand salty tears,
And all the wisdom that the world hath taught.
But in thy eyes I see a different glass,
That shows me not the man that I have been,
But lets the heavy shadows safely pass,
To reveal the spirit that is hid within.
Thy love is like a mirror, clear and bright,
That gives me back a vision of my best,
To guide me through the terrors of the night,
And grant my weary soul a quiet rest.
Though all the world should crumble and decay,
Thy love shall light me on my lonely way.
