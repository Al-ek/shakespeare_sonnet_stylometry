I
When dawn first lifts the curtain of the sky,
And pale gold spills upon the waking field,
My restless thought upon thy form doth lie,
As if thy grace the morning’s light revealed.
The breeze that stirs the tender budding leaves
Repeats thy name in soft uncertain tone;
Each wandering cloud a shadowed image weaves
Of beauty I had thought was thine alone.
Yet time, that subtle thief with patient tread,
Doth mark the bloom that youth so proudly wears;
He lays unseen his fingers on the head
And steals the brightness unawares.
But verse shall build what years cannot destroy—
A deathless home for wonder and for joy.

II
What star was kindled when thine eyes were made,
That all the lesser lights must pale thereby?
The sun itself seems humbled in thy shade,
And seeks some gentler quarter of the sky.
For when thy glance upon the earth is cast,
The stubborn winter loosens from the ground;
And sorrow, which had sworn itself to last,
In thy mild presence dares not make a sound.
Yet fear attends the sweetness I adore,
For fragile things are fortune’s favored prey;
The rose most bright is watched by storms the more,
And fairest summer hastens into gray.
So while thy glory warms my mortal sight,
I bind its echo fast in lines of light.

III
O fleeting hour that crowns thy youthful brow,
Why spend thy riches with so swift a hand?
The mirror waits to tell thee even now
How swift the years pass silent through the land.
Look on thyself, and in that vision see
A treasury no prudent heart would waste;
For beauty hoarded only selfishly
Leaves barren time and memory disgraced.
Let gentle love in living likeness grow,
That future eyes thy borrowed grace may claim;
Thus shall thy bloom in other springs bestow
A second morning to thy cherished name.
Else when thy candle gutters into night,
No heir shall guard the remnant of its light.

IV
The winter wind that combs the withered plain
Seems kin to thoughts that wander far from thee;
For absence spreads a dim and restless pain
Where once thy voice moved bright and easily.
Each path we trod appears a haunted ground,
Where memory stands and will not be dismissed;
The echo of thy step in every sound
Returns like music half-remembered, missed.
Yet hope, a quiet pilgrim of the heart,
Still tends the flame that distance cannot freeze;
Though leagues divide what once was not apart,
Affection walks where once it found its ease.
Thus love, though stretched across uncertain miles,
Finds harbor still within remembered smiles.

V
When pride adorns thee like a shining cloak,
And praise attends thee with too eager tongue,
Beware the seeds within such flattery spoke,
For vanity is cunning though it’s young.
The orchard rich with fruit invites the worm;
The tallest cedar greets the lightning first;
And sweetest springs that in the meadow form
May fail when summer’s heat becomes the worst.
Hold fast instead to modest wisdom’s guide,
Whose quiet strength no sudden wind can break;
For shallow glory ebbs with fortune’s tide,
While humble roots a firmer kingdom make.
So shall thy worth outlive the noisy throng
And ripen deep in memory and in song.

VI
I watched the evening fold the weary day,
And thought of thee beneath the rising moon;
The light that silvered every shadowed way
Seemed but a messenger that comes too soon.
For hadst thou walked beside me in that hour,
No star had dared to claim the greater grace;
The night itself had yielded all its power
To dwell reflected in thy gentle face.
Yet absence paints thee brighter in my mind,
As darkness sharpens every distant spark;
Thus love, though parted, curiously doth find
A richer flame against the thicker dark.
So longing makes a kingdom out of air,
And crowns thy image sovereign ruler there.

VII
The painter strives with careful hand and eye
To fix the wonder nature freely gave;
Yet all his craft beneath thy glance must die,
For art grows timid where thy graces wave.
No marble figure carved by patient years
Could hold the warmth that stirs within thy smile;
The sculptor’s chisel fashions stone, not tears,
Nor breath that lives a living heart the while.
But verse, though humble in its narrow frame,
May wander where the silent statues fail;
Through lines that whisper softly of thy name
It sets thy fleeting presence to a trail.
Thus though the body bends to time’s decree,
These words may keep some shadow yet of thee.

VIII
Sweet music wandering through the twilight air
Once taught my soul a harmony of thine;
For every trembling note seemed half aware
Of echoes answering in thy design.
As chords together shape a richer sound,
So hearts in concord multiply their grace;
Yet single strings when lonely grief has found
Give forth a tone of sorrow in their place.
Why then should love stand solitary still,
When union breeds a sweeter melody?
Let mutual kindness tune the stubborn will
And teach two lives a shared accord to be.
For hearts apart but poorly learn to sing;
Together they become a living string.

IX
The garden sleeps beneath the patient snow,
Yet hidden roots remember summer’s fire;
So too within my quiet thoughts doth grow
The living trace of thy remembered choir.
Though silence now usurps the laughing hour,
And frost commands the branches stiff and bare,
Affection waits like some enduring flower
Whose secret bud defies the colder air.
Then when the kindly sun returns once more,
And rivers loose their silver tongues again,
The heart shall open as it did before
And cast away the long, reluctant pain.
For love, though chilled by fortune’s winter breath,
Keeps spring concealed beneath the mask of death.

X
If harsh reproach should fall upon thy name,
As envious tongues delight to wound the fair,
Let patience answer where they kindle blame,
And truth defend thee with its quiet care.
For slander, like a gust that clouds the day,
Soon spends its rage and leaves the heavens clear;
But steady virtue, walking in its way,
Outlives the rumor sharpened by a sneer.
The mirror of a faithful conscience bright
Needs not the borrowed polish praise supplies;
It holds within a self-sustaining light
That mocks the shadows cast by hostile eyes.
Thus time shall prove what malice dared deny—
Thy worth stands firm though idle voices cry.

XI
What strange alchemy in love is wrought,
That simple glances weigh like crowns of gold?
The smallest token by thy favor brought
Outvalues gems that greedy kings might hold.
A word from thee becomes a lasting feast
Where memory returns with eager tread;
And even silence, if by thee released,
Seems full of meanings delicately spread.
So slight the cause that wakes this deep delight,
Yet vast the kingdom where its banners wave;
For love expands the narrow bounds of sight
And finds a treasure common eyes would waive.
Thus hearts transformed by passion’s gentle art
Discover worlds concealed in one dear heart.

XII
I marked today the shadows lengthen slow
Across the meadow’s patient, fading green;
The sinking sun performed its quiet show
And closed the golden page that once had been.
So too the vigor of our early years
Slides westward through the calendar of breath;
We gather wisdom watered oft by tears
While nearing still the silent shore of death.
Yet if thy presence walk beside my own,
The path grows lighter though the dusk increase;
For love converts the hardest mile of stone
Into a road that gently leads to peace.
Thus age, though stern, shall lose its bitter art
If twilight finds thee dwelling in my heart.

XIII
Think not thy beauty merely thine to keep,
As if the rose might claim the sun alone;
The charm thou wearest wakes a sacred sweep
Of joy wherever kindly eyes are thrown.
For heaven lends its treasures but a while
And asks that mortal hands extend their grace;
The generous look, the freely given smile,
Make bright the common pathways of the race.
So spend thy wealth of spirit without fear,
Nor hoard the light that fortune bade thee bear;
The candle that illumines far and near
Finds not its flame diminished by the share.
Thus giving multiplies the gift we hold,
And turns brief beauty into living gold.

XIV
No star I read, nor chart of wandering spheres,
Can teach me half the truth thy presence shows;
The heavens write their wisdom through the years,
Yet clearer still the lesson in thee grows.
For in thy face a constancy appears
That mocks the fickle dance of astral fire;
The orbit of thy kindness, free of fears,
Moves by a law no treacherous skies conspire.
Thus while the planets stray through doubt and flame,
My faith is fixed where mortal grace abides;
Not distant lights but thy enduring frame
Becomes the compass by which love resides.
So though the firmament dissolve above,
My course remains directed still by love.

XV
When first ambition stirred within my breast,
I sought the loud applause of crowded halls;
But one soft word from thee outweighed the rest
And stilled the echo ringing in those walls.
For glory shouted by the restless throng
Is like the surf that breaks and fades away;
Yet gentle praise that breathes in quiet song
Remains when louder triumphs lose their sway.
So have I learned that honor’s truest part
Lives not in noise nor pageantry of might,
But in the silent witness of the heart
That knows thy favor as its guiding light.
Thus fame itself grows humble in thy sight,
Content to share the margin of thy light.

XVI
The summer fields once glowed with lavish hue,
Now stoop beneath the scythe of patient time;
So mortal strength, though vigorous and new,
Must bow at last beneath the years’ slow climb.
Yet something stubborn in the human frame
Refuses wholly to be overthrown;
For thought erects a monument of name
Where flesh had feared to wander all alone.
Therefore these lines attempt a fragile stay
Against the tide that bears all forms away.
If they but keep one faint remembered ray
Of thee when both our voices cannot stay,
Then time himself, though proud of his domain,
Shall find some small rebellion in this strain.

XVII
How poor the tongue that would thy worth proclaim,
For praise grows timid near so bright a theme;
Each word I weigh seems unworthy of thy name,
A coin too light to purchase what I deem.
Yet silence worse betrays the love I bear,
For unsaid truth lies buried in the breast;
Thus trembling speech attempts the daring air
Though conscious of its poverty confessed.
Perhaps some future age with kinder ear
May read these lines and think them nobly meant;
If so, thy shadow shall again appear
Within the humble tribute here I lent.
Then though my verse be frail and poorly wrought,
Thy merit lifts the value of the thought.

XVIII
Not every storm that gathers breaks in rain;
Some pass like threats that vanish in the blue.
So many fears that troubled heart and brain
Dissolve when once I turn my eyes to you.
Your calm rebukes the tumults of the mind,
As quiet harbors tame the sailor’s dread;
Where once wild doubts blew hard as winter wind,
Your steady kindness gently walks instead.
Thus courage grows not always born of might,
But from the nearness of a faithful soul;
Your presence lends the faltering spirit sight
And draws the scattered fragments to a whole.
So strength, which once I sought in distant lands,
Was waiting simply in your open hands.

XIX
O subtle Time, whose fingers never tire
Of tracing wrinkles on the fairest brow,
You boast the ruin of each bright desire
And bend proud towers beneath your patient plow.
Yet know there lives a stubborn craft in men
That steals a fragment from your greedy store;
For ink and thought conspire with careful pen
To guard what flesh alone could keep no more.
You may erase the blossom from the field,
And dim the brightness dwelling in the eye;
But lines once shaped refuse to wholly yield
And keep a whisper when the forms all die.
So rage, old thief—your triumph is not whole;
These verses keep one portrait of a soul.

XX
If ever doubt should ask what love has done,
Or what reward attends its constant fire,
Let this small record answer, line by one,
That faithful hearts may reach a fairer spire.
For though no crown nor kingdom crowns the deed,
Affection builds a realm no sword can claim;
Its quiet victories outgrow the seed
Of louder conquests trumpeted by fame.
Thus having loved, the poorest life is rich,
And having shared, the loneliest path grows wide;
The humble cottage rivals palace niche
When two consenting spirits there abide.
So ends my song: if truth within it move,
The final word and first remains still—love.

XXI
When dawn in amber robes the east doth climb,
And night’s black mantle looseth from the air,
My thoughts, like larks, ascend in hopeful rhyme,
To sing of thee, whose grace outshines the fair.
Thy eyes, twin stars that shame the vault of heaven,
Do guide my course through tempests of despair;
Thy voice, a chord by gentlest angels given,
Doth soothe the griefs no mortal else could bear.
Yet time, that thief who steals both bloom and breath,
With stealthy pace makes fragile all delight;
He whispers low the certainty of death,
And dims the glory kindled by thy sight.
So while thou livest, let my verse proclaim
A deathless echo of thy lovely name.

XXII
As summer’s pride yields up to autumn’s claim,
And golden fields to barren frost resign,
So fades the flush that once adorned thy frame,
Though still thy spirit glows in form divine.
What art hath time, that dares such spoils to make
Of beauty wrought with such celestial care?
No fortress stands that he will not o’ertake,
Nor jewel bright that he will choose to spare.
Yet in my breast thy image stands secure,
Unshaken by the seasons’ cruel decree;
For love, though flesh be frail, shall aye endure,
And carve thy likeness deep in memory.
Let time do worst—my verse shall still defend
Thy grace from ruin, and outlive his end.

XXIII
When silent night enthrones her sable queen,
And bids the world in shadowed rest to lie,
My wakeful thoughts revisit scenes once seen
Where thou and I did share love’s harmony.
Each whispered vow returns upon the breeze,
Each glance rekindles fires long thought to fade;
In memory’s court, I find a tender ease
Though time hath cast our joys in distant shade.
Yet grief attends such visions of delight,
For what was mine now lives but in recall;
The sweetest dream grows bitter in its flight,
When waking proves it shadows after all.
Still let me dream, though truth be sharp and cold,
For dreams preserve what time hath made too old.

XXIV
Why doth my pen refuse its noble task,
When thou, its muse, dost bid it sing thy praise?
What richer theme could any poet ask
Than beauty crowned with virtue’s gentle rays?
Yet words fall short, as though ashamed to rise
Against the glory they would fain express;
For language faints beneath thy radiant eyes,
And yields before thy perfect comeliness.
Thus must I write, though knowing well my art
But limps behind the splendor it would chase;
For love compels the labor of my heart,
Though skill be weak to shadow forth thy grace.
Accept the will, though poor the gift appear,
For love writes best when love itself is clear.

XXV
Let others boast of lineage or of gold,
Or victories won in fortune’s fickle game;
Their borrowed pride is brittle, faint, and cold,
And fades as swiftly as it first became.
But I, more rich than kings in jeweled array,
Possess a treasure none can take away:
Thy love, more constant than the break of day,
Doth make my low estate seem high as they.
What though the world deny me rank or place?
What though no herald speaks my humble name?
Thy favor grants a far more noble grace,
And lights my soul with everlasting flame.
Thus stand I crowned, though clad in poor attire,
A monarch made by love’s enduring fire.

XXVI
If I could bind the hours with chains of gold,
And hold them fast from their relentless flight,
Then would thy youth remain as now behold,
Untouched by age, unshadowed by the night.
But time runs free, and mocks all mortal plea,
With wingèd heels that never pause nor tire;
He laughs at vows of immortality,
And feeds on all that beauty may inspire.
Yet though thy form must yield to his command,
Thy essence shall no tyranny obey;
For in my verse thy truth shall ever stand,
And bloom anew with each returning day.
So shall thy life, though fleeting in the flesh,
In deathless lines forever be made fresh.

XXVII
In absence’ school I learn love’s truest art,
Where longing sharpens every fond desire;
Distance, though cruel, instructs the faithful heart
To prize the flame it cannot yet acquire.
Each mile between us stretches out the thread
That binds my soul more firmly unto thine;
Each hour apart renews the tears I shed,
Yet proves my love more constant by design.
For ease breeds sloth, and plenty dulls delight,
But want keeps passion ever keen and strong;
Thus do I bless the day and curse the night
That makes me yearn for thee so deep and long.
Come back, dear love, and end this sweet unrest,
For longing’s joy is pain when not redressed.

XXVIII
The moon, pale watcher of the lover’s grief,
Beholds my vigil through the sleepless hours;
She lends no comfort, brings me no relief,
But mirrors sorrow in her waning powers.
The stars, like scattered hopes across the sky,
Do flicker faintly in their distant spheres;
They seem to mock the tears within mine eye,
As though delight were born of others’ tears.
O night, thou art too long when love is far,
And every minute stretches into years;
No rest I find beneath thy dusky star,
But feed my heart with absence and with fears.
Yet dawn shall come, and with it hope anew,
For every night must yield its light to you.

XXIX
When fortune frowns and casts me from her grace,
And men regard me with a scornful eye,
I curse my fate and weep my lowly place,
And wish my state more rich, more fair, more high.
But straight I think on thee, and all is changed;
My spirit soars above the earthbound throng,
My griefs grow light, my sorrows are estranged,
And joy returns as though it ne’er were gone.
For in thy love I find a wealth untold,
A kingdom richer than all crowns can bring;
No coin of gold could match the worth I hold,
When I am thine, and thou my sovereign king.
Thus in thy thought I rise from depths of care,
And envy none who breathe the richer air.

XXX
What reckoning shall I make with vanished days,
When memory calls each loss to fresh account?
The ghosts of joys once lit with golden rays
Return, and bid my hidden griefs surmount.
I mourn anew the friends long since departed,
The loves that slipped like sand from eager hands;
Each sorrow wakes, though long ago it started,
And stands before me as if still it stands.
Yet in this audit of my past despair,
One comfort shines, a light that will not fade:
Thy love restores what time seemed to impair,
And pays the debts that grief and loss have made.
Thus all my tears are sweeter when I see
They lead me still, at last, to solace thee.

XXXI
Thou art the sum of all I hold most dear,
The gathered worth of every love I’ve known;
In thee, their scattered virtues reappear,
As though their grace were planted in one throne.
Each friend I’ve lost, each joy that passed away,
Finds resurrection in thy living form;
Their echoes speak in all thou dost convey,
Their warmth survives within thy spirit warm.
Thus art thou more than single soul to me,
But many hearts in one fair body bound;
A living treasury of memory,
Where all my dearest affections are found.
So loving thee, I love what time hath slain,
And find in loss a rich and sweet regain.

XXXII
If thou survive me when I am but dust,
And read these lines that time shall yet preserve,
Judge not too harshly what I wrote in trust,
Though skill may falter where my heart had nerve.
For I, in infancy of verse, did strive
To honor thee with all the art I knew;
But later pens more finely may contrive
To sing thy worth with phrases fresh and true.
Then grant me this: that though my words be weak,
My love was strong, and sought thee faithfully;
And if my praise seem poor for what I seek,
Yet let my truth excuse the poverty.
So shall I live, though buried long before,
In thy kind thoughts, remembered evermore.

XXXIII
Full oft the sun begins the day in gold,
With beams that kiss the mountain’s lofty brow;
Yet soon a cloud his splendor dares to hold,
And dims the glory that adorned him now.
So too thy favor once did shine on me,
With warmth that lit my world in radiant grace;
But now some shadow veils that light I see,
And casts a chill upon thy altered face.
Yet blame I not the sun for fleeting cloud,
Nor thee for change that may not be thy will;
For nature’s course no mortal may avow,
And hearts must bend where fate commands them still.
Though dimmed, thy light remains my guiding ray,
And leads me on, though clouds obscure the day.

XXXIV
Why didst thou promise fair and then withdraw,
Like April skies that shift from bright to rain?
Thy change hath left me bound in sorrow’s law,
To feel the sting of hope now turned to pain.
Yet tears thou giv’st, and with them sorrow’s balm,
For thy remorse doth soften what was hard;
Thy sighs restore a measure of the calm
That once thy breach of faith did wholly mar.
Though fault was thine, thy pity wins me still,
And love forgives where reason might condemn;
For hearts that truly feel another’s ill
Will gladly heal, though hurt return to them.
Thus do I pardon, not because thou’rt right,
But love sees more than justice in its sight.

XXXV
No rose without its thorn may truly bloom,
Nor light exist untouched by shadow’s claim;
So must I bear thy faults, though they consume
The perfect image once I held thy name.
For who is free from blemish in this life?
What soul escapes the stain of some misdeed?
To judge too harshly breeds a needless strife,
Where mercy might more fruitful love concede.
Thus do I cloak thy errors in my care,
And make excuses where none may suffice;
For love is blind to faults it will not share,
And sees but virtue through forgiving eyes.
Yet take thou heed, lest patience wear away,
For even love may tire if pressed too far astray.

XXXVI
Though we be twain, our hearts are joined as one,
Yet must we walk as strangers in the light;
For reasons born of circumstance undone
Compel us hide what gives our souls delight.
Our love, though true, must dwell in secret shade,
Unspoken, lest the world should cast it down;
Thus are we forced to wear a careful trade,
And mask with silence what should earn a crown.
But though apart in outward show we stand,
Within, no distance can our bond divide;
Each pulse of mine beats answer to thy hand,
And holds thee near, though far from public pride.
So let the world believe what it may see—
Our truth resides where none but we may be.

XXXVII
As aged sire delights in youthful son,
And sees renewed the strength he once possessed,
So do I find in thee what I have done
In better days, now faded from my breast.
Thy vigor lends me life I thought was spent,
Thy grace restores the pride I once had known;
Through thee my former self finds fresh ascent,
And claims again a glory not its own.
Thus do I live in thee, though time hath worn
My powers thin and robbed me of my prime;
Thy youth becomes the spring where I am torn
Between remembrance and the hand of time.
So long as thou art fair and strong and free,
Some part of youth remains alive in me.

XXXVIII
What muse could ask for richer source than thee,
Whose very being breeds a thousand songs?
Thy presence is a well of poetry,
From which each thirsty line its verse prolongs.
No need have I to seek in distant lands
For inspiration rare or visions bright;
Thy look supplies what all my art demands,
And fills my page with wonder and delight.
Yet still I fear my verse falls short of truth,
And cannot match the glory it would praise;
For thou exceed’st all measure, form, and proof,
And art too vast for mortal words to raise.
Still will I write, though knowing all I do
Is but a shadow cast by light of you.

XXXIX
O how to praise thee, being part of me,
When self and subject mingle into one?
If I extol thy worth, it seems to be
Myself I crown for what thou hast begun.
Thus must we part in verse, though joined in heart,
That I may give thee honor due and right;
For distance lends the poet’s voice its art,
And grants the space wherein to frame delight.
So stand apart, that I may better see
The full perfection of thy separate grace;
And in that space, describe more faithfully
The charm no nearness wholly can embrace.
Yet though divided thus for praise’s sake,
Our love remains no artifice can break.

XL
Take all my loves, my love, yea, take them all—
What hast thou then more than thou hadst before?
No wealth is lost though I resign their thrall,
For thou wert all their meaning at the core.
If thou dost wrong me, still I cannot hate,
For love commands a gentler kind of will;
Though injury would stir a harsher fate,
My heart submits, obedient to thee still.
Yet in this yielding lies a quiet power,
For patience proves a strength beyond offense;
And love that bends may yet outlast the hour
When wrath consumes both justice and defense.
So take, and take again, what I call mine—
For all I have was ever truly thine.
