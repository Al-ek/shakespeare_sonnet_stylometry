I

When time doth lay his finger on the brow
Of she whose beauty summer hath confessed,
Shall I not cry against his cruel vow
That robs the fairest rose of all the rest?
For in her eyes the morning finds its glass,
And every dawn doth borrow light from her,
Yet all fair things must wither as the grass
When autumn's cold doth through the meadows stir.
But verse shall be the vault where beauty sleeps,
Impervious to the frost that kills the flower,
And what the grave in darkness blindly keeps,
My lines shall give again in deathless hour.
  So long as men draw breath and eyes can see,
  Thy face shall live immortal here in me.

II

Thou art the season that I cannot name,
Not spring nor autumn, neither cold nor warm,
But something past the reach of nature's frame,
A stillness at the center of the storm.
When I am far from thee the hours grow vast
And every minute doubles in its weight,
As ships becalmed upon a windless mast
Must hang between their longing and their fate.
But when thy presence fills the narrow room
The very walls lean inward, glad to hear
Thy voice that drives the winter from its gloom
And makes of every month the turning year.
  What art thou then, that season without name?
  Thou art my world—and I thy foolish flame.

III

Look in the mirror and confess to thee
What thou wouldst hide from all the watching world:
That beauty is a debt, and being free
Thou hast not paid it back with love unfurled.
Thy mother once was young as thou art now,
And in her face thy face was first designed,
She gave thee all: the cheekbone and the brow,
The gentle wit, the quick and curious mind.
So shalt thou give, or else those gifts must die
When thou art gone and none shall take thine art,
No child shall look upon the winter sky
With eyes that hold the warmth of thine own heart.
  The world is but a glass that shows the past;
  Give back thy gifts, that they may ever last.

IV

How often have I cursed the fading light
That steals the hours before my work is done,
And railed against the early closing night
That cuts me off from thee before begun?
For day is but the promise of thy face,
And all the business of the waking hours
Is but a long and stumbling, graceless race
Toward thee, the end of all my hopeful powers.
Yet night itself, when thou art lying near,
Becomes a kingdom richer than the day,
And darkness is no darkness when I hear
Thy breathing soft upon the pillow's grey.
  So cursed is light that keeps me from thy side,
  And blessed dark in which my heart doth bide.

V

What summer hoards, the jealous frost shall claim,
And all the gold that filled the meadow's cup
Shall answer to December's bitter aim
When harvest hath been drunk and given up.
For time doth keep no covenant with fair,
Nor seal his mercy on the blooming cheek,
But strips the bough of all it once did wear
And leaves the shivering branch both pale and weak.
Yet beauty hath a remedy in kind:
If she that owns it lend it to a child,
Then what the hand of winter leaves behind
Shall bloom again in features undefiled.
  So yield thy gifts to love before they pass,
  And see thyself renewed in beauty's glass.

VI

Shall I compare thee to a winter fire
That warms the room but cannot warm the soul?
Or art thou rather some unnamed desire
That makes of every half a trembling whole?
I have seen beauty in the open field
When bluebells nod in unison with wind,
And watched how willows to the river yield
As lovers yield to what they most have sinned.
But none of these hath held me like thy gaze
That falls on me and fixes me in place,
As amber holds the creature of past days
Suspended in the amber of thy grace.
  Compare thee? No. All comparison must fail.
  Thou art the truth whereof all else is tale.

VII

When morning lifts her lantern o'er the hill
And gilds the world with promises of gold,
The creatures of the field stand hushed and still
To watch that ancient pageantry unfold.
So thou, when first thou enter'st any room,
Dost draw all eyes as candles draw the moth,
And faces lift from shadow and from gloom
As fields receive the sun-drenched morning cloth.
But morning, having climbed to her high place,
Doth find by noon the world hath turned away,
And all who praised the glory of her face
Find new devotions as she dips toward grey.
  Then use thy morning well before the fall,
  For borrowed light at last returneth all.

VIII

Why doth sweet sound make sorrowful thine ear
When harmony should set the spirit free?
The lute that weeps hath still a note most clear,
And concord sings what silence fails to see.
Consider how the choir of many parts
Doth bind its separate voices into one,
As scattered embers, drawn to kindred hearts,
Compose a single flame against the sun.
The instrument that hangs unplucked on high
Doth waste its music in the empty air,
And all its latent song must fade and die
Unless some willing hand doth tune it there.
  So thou, who hast a song yet left unsung,
  Must find thy consort now, while yet thou'rt young.

IX

How careful was I when I took in hand
The tender project of thy early love,
As one who builds upon uncertain sand
And prays for mercy from the skies above.
For love first spoken is a fragile thing,
Like moth-wing patterns pressed upon the air,
A breath may break it, and the words we bring
May serve to wound the very heart laid bare.
Yet I was bold, and boldness found its grace,
For thou didst meet my faltering words with thine,
And in the meeting of our gaze and face
I drank thee off like consecrated wine.
  So blessed is boldness when the heart is true,
  And twice so blessed when it is met by you.

X

Wilt thou deny that thou hast love to spare
For one beyond thyself, yet turn aside?
To hoard one's heart is a most grievous snare,
And loveless thrift but swells the self with pride.
Consider: when the miser grips his gold
And will not spend it where the needy stand,
The coin grows cold, the story is soon told,
And wealth decays in the ungiving hand.
So beauty kept from love's appointed use
Doth rot the very bloom it means to save,
And she who will not let affection loose
Doth dig for love a solitary grave.
  Then be not cruel in thy beauty's reign;
  What thou withholdst in joy returns in pain.

XI

O that thou wert as thou once wert to me,
Before the world had written on thy face
Those lines of care that now I barely see
Through the fond habit of familiar grace.
I do not mourn the lines—they are thine own,
And every crease of laughter round thine eyes
Is dearer to me than the seed first sown
Of beauty fresh beneath unopened skies.
But I do mourn the distance time hath made
Between the you who loved me without thought
And this more careful you, who art afraid
To give what once so freely thou hadst brought.
  Come back to me across that narrow space,
  And let us be again what time would erase.

XII

How shall I mark the turning of the year
When every season steals some cherished thing?
The violet bows its head upon the bier
And yields the field to autumn's withering.
I see the oak grow gaunt against the sky
Where once it wore its canopy of green,
And all the bright-eyed summer days gone by
Lie pressed like flowers where summer once had been.
When I behold thee in thy flourishing
I think of harvests gathered, then forgot,
Of how the thrush grows quiet after spring
And sings no more when summer finds her not.
  Yet I shall hold thee past the season's art:
  Time takes the face, but not what's in the heart.

XIII

The absent heart is not the faithless one,
Though distance lends itself to doubt's designs,
As shadows multiply when sets the sun
And one frail tree becomes a thousand pines.
I have been gone from thee these many weeks
And all the while thy face before me burned,
A lantern that the homesick traveler seeks
When he hath strayed and longs to be returned.
Doubt thou the stars are fire if thou must,
Doubt that the sun doth move, doubt truth a lie,
But never doubt that in my every gust
Of longing breath thy name is my first cry.
  The farthest distance only serves to prove
  How close thou art in the geography of love.

XIV

My knowledge comes not from the turning spheres,
Nor do I read my fortune in the sky,
No augur's art hath schooled these later years
To tell me whether good or ill draws nigh.
The comet's blaze reveals no truth to me,
Nor can I parse what planetary sign
Doth govern what shall come or cease to be,
Or bend the will of mortal to divine.
Yet in thy countenance I read most clear
What no astronomer hath reckoned yet:
That where thou art, there beauty need not fear,
And truth hath found a face it shan't forget.
  But if thou turn from both, my stars go dark,
  And beauty's age is ended with thy spark.

XV

A different autumn settles now in me,
Not that loud season gilt with harvest gold,
But something quieter, a falling free
Of all the urgencies that made me bold.
I see it in the mirror's morning face,
Where time hath penned his marginal critique,
And left his silver notings in the place
Where once my darker certainties would speak.
Yet do not pity what thou seest there,
For autumn hath its dignities and ease,
A slower warmth, a more considered care,
A sweeter fruit that hangs on yellowed trees.
  So love me in this season of the year:
  What summer promised, autumn shall make clear.

XVI

What stronger weapon hast thou than this verse
To hold thy beauty from the tyrant's claim?
Though I may carve thee into every terse
And labored line, the carving is not fame.
The fairest portrait hung in gallery halls
Doth only hold the surface of the eye,
And painted beauty on the painter's walls
Doth smile the same as summer hurtles by.
But thou couldst give thyself in living kind
And leave behind a warmer, truer art,
Some child whose eye doth carry forth thy mind,
And beat thy rhythm in a younger heart.
  No pen of mine can do what nature can:
  Live on through love, and not through any man.

XVII

What future reader, turning these thin leaves,
Shall credit what I set so plainly here?
That beauty such as thine the eye conceives
But once, if fortune lend the lucky year?
They'll say I dealt in a fantastical trade,
That I exaggerated every grace,
That no such living creature could have made
So wild an argument of form and face.
And I shall not be there to swear it true,
Nor bring them to the light where thou dost stand,
And bid them look an hour or two on you
And tell me then my praise was merely planned.
  So let some child of thine outlive this page,
  And prove my foolish verse was not old age.

XVIII

The reckless youth who squanders all his light
On pleasures brief and merrymaking vain
Shall wake one morning to a greyer sight
And count the cost of all that careless gain.
Not so with thee, who hast been careful, wise,
Who hast set love aside like winter stores
Against the season when the cold wind cries
And frost comes begging at the bolted doors.
But care too great is cousin to despair,
And prudence kept too long becomes a chain,
The miser guards his gold with equal prayer
As he who throws it to the summer rain.
  So be not over-thrifty with thy heart:
  Spend freely now, before the long depart.

XIX

Thou, time, hast felled the cedar and the oak,
And turned the river from its ancient bed,
And wrapped the waking world in winter's cloak
When all the flowers wished for warmth instead.
Thou hast unmade the faces of the great
And worn the lettered stone to nameless smooth,
And made of every kingdom desolate
What once no siege of war could dare to move.
Go then, do all the damage that thou wilt
To hill and valley, season, stone, and tide,
But leave untouched the beauty thou hast built
In her for whom these trembling lines are tried.
  Do what thou wilt to all the world beside,
  But lay no ruin where my love doth bide.

XX

So now I lay this little book of hours
Before thee like a garland, roughly made
From such poor blossoms as my garden flowers,
More weed than rose, more stubborn than afraid.
If any line hath caught the light aright,
If any phrase hath held what I have meant,
Then I am grateful for that borrowed light
And count my crooked effort well-spent.
For thou hast been my subject and my cause,
My north star and my wandering southern wind,
The text upon which every poem draws
Its meaning, like a lamp that lights the blind.
  Forgive what falls short of what thou art:
  No words were ever equal to this heart.

XXI

When golden light doth fade from summer's brow,
And autumn strips the elm of verdant dress,
What hand shall stay the season's breaking vow,
Or bid the rose resist time's cold caress?
The hours do march as soldiers sworn to war,
Each footfall wearing thin the tender ground,
And beauty, once a lamp, now scarce a star,
Is swallowed in the dark without a sound.
Yet thou remainest fixed within my thought,
A portrait hung where neither damp nor flame
May reach to ruin what thy presence wrought,
Nor any season dare to blur thy name.
  So long as memory breathes within this chest,
  Thou art the season I love best.

XXII

My glass doth show a face grown thin with years,
The furrows ploughed by sorrow's patient share,
Yet I count not the evidence it bears,
For thou hast made me richer than I wear.
What boots it if the brow grow silver-grey,
If in thine eyes I find no fault therein?
The heart knoweth not that it doth decay
When love is the bright salve applied to sin.
The mirror lies that sees but flesh and bone
And numbers up the ruin of each year;
True wealth is not in what the glass hath shown,
But in the warmth of what thou keepest near.
  Then let the glass reflect what time hath done;
  My face is old, but thou art still my sun.

XXIII

As one who steps upon a frozen lake
And hears the ice protest beneath his weight,
So I approach thy silence and must make
My passage slow, lest I should tempt my fate.
I have not words enough to say the whole
Of what thy presence summons from my blood,
The way thou fill'st each corner of my soul
As April rain doth fill the winter wood.
Then let these lines speak what my lips deny,
And carry to thee what my tongue would keep,
That thou might read in verse what I'd not cry
Aloud for fear the moment were too deep.
  Accept this poem as a lover's deed,
  The only tongue I trust to plant the seed.

XXIV

The miser counts his coin by candlelight
And sleeps upon his treasure, cold and proud,
But I, grown rich upon a fairer sight,
Need no such hoard beneath no such a shroud.
Thy smile is currency no mint hath made,
Thy laughter is the interest I seek,
And in thine absence I am sore afraid
My purse grows lean, my fortune growing weak.
Then do not stint thyself from my poor sight,
Nor keep thy countenance locked from my gain;
Let me be bankrupt in thy present light,
Than wealthy in a world thou dost not grace again.
  For all the gold that ever Tiber bore
  Is nothing to thy face, and nothing more.

XXV

What doth it profit one to claim the stars
If those same stars grow cold and dark by day?
Or boast of conquest earned in distant wars
While all the bloom of living fades away?
I see great men who strut the public square
With title stitched in gold upon the breast,
Yet love hath looked at them with vacant stare,
For they have traded warmth to pass some test.
Give me instead the small unpraised estate
Of lying close to thee on winter nights,
No herald blows a trumpet at my gate,
Yet I am richer than those titled knights.
  For all their banners in the morning air,
  They have not what I have when thou art there.

XXVI

Shall I compare the compass of my love
To that small circle drawn about the heart?
The one cannot contain what lies above
The border of all science and all art.
A surgeon knows the organ, cold and sure,
And charts its chambers with a studied hand,
But what affliction is there and what cure
When love outspreads all he may understand?
My love is not a patient on a bed
To be examined, measured, and explained;
It is the current swift, the wine blood-red,
The fever that must burn before it's waned.
  Then ask not what my love is or how wide;
  Only know that thou art all inside.

XXVII

The pilgrim walks a thousand stony miles
And carries on his back all he may need,
He does not pause for comfort or for smiles
But presses on compelled by faithful creed.
Such is the journey of this heart for thee,
Across the cold and unforgiving ground,
Past doubt that spreads its branches like a tree
Whose roots would keep the weary traveller bound.
I shall not stop for winter's argument
Nor turn aside at pleasure's gilded door,
But keep the face of love my fixed intent
Till I arrive upon thy welcoming shore.
  Then let the road be hard and let it long;
  The destination makes the traveller strong.

XXVIII

When night descends and all the household sleeps,
And candles drown themselves in their own wax,
I find my waking mind the vigil keeps
Of thee, and will not grant me my relax.
What strange employment hath this restless thought
That spends the dark in cataloguing thee,
Each feature from my memory is brought
And laid before the sleepless eye to see?
The brow, the hand, the voice that haunts the hall
Of all my quiet hours with its sound,
I am thy prisoner though thou'rt not my wall,
Thou'rt the loose chain by which I am still bound.
  Then bind me harder, love, or set me free;
  This half-captivity is agony.

XXIX

When I do count the hours since last we met
And find the sum more grievous than I'd thought,
I fall to cursing what I would forget
And rage against the distance time hath wrought.
Then doth the envious mind begin to roam
And find in other men what I lack most:
The ease, the charm, the comfortable home,
The steady fire where I have but a ghost.
Yet in this valley of my discontent
There rises like a crocus through the frost
The thought of thee, and all my sullen bent
Is turned to joy as if no joy were lost.
  For thinking thee is wealth enough for me;
  In that one thought I am entirely free.

XXX

When to the quiet of a winter church
I bring the burden of my wasted years,
And through the archive of my past do search
And find it full of nothing but arrears,
I mourn the days that perished without cause,
The friendships spent and never well repaid,
The chances lost that gave me no applause
Except the hollow kind that quickly fade.
But as I sit within that echoing nave
And count the losses folded in the past,
I find thy name engraved upon the stave
Of every joy that was designed to last.
  And so the grieving of old griefs doth end,
  In thee I find the sum of what I'd spend.

XXXI

Thou art the harbour when the tempest raves
And all the rigging screams its fraying thread,
The steady light above the breaking waves
That guides me home when every hope seems dead.
I have been blown by passions not my own
And driven to the shoals of dark despair,
Have wrecked upon the rocks and sunk alone
And thought the world had nothing left to spare.
But thou hast been the constant in the storm,
The star that would not shift its place for cloud,
The cove that offered shelter, dry and warm,
The voice that called me back above the shroud.
  What storm can shake me while thy light doth burn?
  I lose my way only that I might return.

XXXII

Doth not the sun each morning make his vow
To climb the sky and give his light away,
And never in his generosity grow sour
Nor ask the flowers to repay the day?
So thou dost shed thy kindness without count
And warm the cold and cheer the heavy-browed,
Drawing from some inexhaustible fount
A grace that never grows pinched or proud.
I wonder at thee as the poor man stands
Before the cathedral's gilded western door,
Amazed that such a thing, wrought by such hands,
Should stand so freely open to the poor.
  Thou art that door, thou art that western light;
  I stand amazed and grateful at thy sight.

XXXIII

The fault I find in time is not that he
Doth take the rose before the autumn's close,
Nor that he dims the eye and bends the knee
And tugs the curtain down on all our shows.
His truest fault is not in what he steals
From beauty's treasury while beauty sleeps,
But in the clumsy haste with which he deals
And never pauses for the vow love keeps.
He rushed us past the spring before we knew
How brief the flowering of those days would be,
And now I search the calendar for you
In every season, finding none so free.
  Then curse thee, time, not for what thou hast taken,
  But for the sleep in which I was forsaken.

XXXIV

What is the worth of beauty unobserved,
The nightingale that sings to empty dark,
The feast prepared for guests who never served
Their presence, leaving cold each candle's mark?
So hast thou been too often left unseen,
Thy graces catalogued by no one's eye,
The garden tended that has ever been
Walked past by those too hurried to draw nigh.
Then let this verse be those belated eyes
That pause at last to take thee truly in,
To see in thee what no poor words suffice
But words must try, though they be rough and thin.
  Beauty demands a witness or it grieves;
  I am thy witness, though I lack thy leaves.

XXXV

No more the moon may claim dominion o'er
The tides of this heart swollen with thy name;
The moon herself grows jealous at the shore
Where thou hast marked the boundary of my claim.
I have found fault before with those who loved
Too wildly and too without compass steered,
But love hath come and left me less unmoved
Than I had thought, and closer than I feared.
Then I am guilty of the very crime
I once judged harshly from a safer seat,
And find that love hath made a fool of time
And made a fool of me, which is more meet.
  Forgive me then the arrogance of youth;
  Love is the cost of learning any truth.

XXXVI

Though we must wear the habit of the day
And walk apart as custom doth require,
No ordinance of man can quite allay
The underground and unreported fire.
The world shall see two figures on a street
Who pass each other with a formal grace,
But what the world doth never see to greet
Is what doth pass between us face to face.
In that swift moment is a country born
With laws and seasons all its own alone,
No parliament may legislate its morn,
No charter claim it for the public's own.
  So let the world its careful business mind;
  We keep our kingdom where no spy may find.

XXXVII

As age doth bring its tally to my door
And reckons up the tax upon my lease,
I find I grieve for what I knew before
When limbs were quick and mornings came with ease.
Yet in this audit of declining days
I find one line that sits beyond all debt:
The memory of thee in younger ways
Before the frost had claimed its cold duet.
That I have loved thee is the one clear sum
That stands though all the other figures fail,
The constant that remains when numbers come
To nothing, and the auditors grow pale.
  Let time collect his due from every part;
  He hath no jurisdiction on the heart.

XXXVIII

How many poets, burning at their desks
By guttered tallow in the winter black,
Have sought for words worthy of their requests
And found the language always stepping back?
The rose is called a rose a thousand times
And still the rose outpaces what it's called;
The lover strains the leash of all his rhymes
And still the beloved stands unscaled, unwalled.
I join that long procession of the failed
Who took their pens to mountains they'd not climb,
And if my verse in its ambition paled,
Then blame the subject, not the threadbare rhyme.
  Thou art the cause of every poem's fall;
  No verse is great enough, or ever shall.

XXXIX

When thou art far and all the miles between
Have swallowed up the sound of thy dear voice,
I furnish up my mind with what I've seen
And make of memory a second choice.
The hand that touched my hand in the old spring
Is held again in the theatre of the mind;
The word thou spoke'st still has its echoing
In chambers where no silence is unkind.
Thus do I in thy absence keep thee near
And cheat the distance of its full account,
For though thou art not bodily here,
The ghost of thee outspreads all love's amount.
  In memory thou livest clear and whole;
  Distance takes the body, not the soul.

XL

Take all I have, take even this poor verse
That limps toward thee on its uneven feet,
Take what is best in me and what is worse
And find in all of it thy nature's seat.
For all I am hath been in some way made
Or shaped or altered by the light of thee,
Each thought I think is of thy making laid
Upon the loom that thou hast set for me.
Then call not what I offer here a gift
If gifts are things that pass from hand to hand;
This is no transfer but a simple lift
Of what is thine back to thy native land.
  I give thee nothing that was ever mine;
  All I return to thee was always thine.
