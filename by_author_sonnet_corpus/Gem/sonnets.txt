I

When morning’s silver light begins to fade,
And autumn’s hand prepares the year for rest,
The vibrant bloom that once the sun obeyed,
Must yield its crown and sink within the chest.
The wandering stream that sought the distant sea,
Is bound by frost and silenced in its race,
So shall the pulse of youth soon cease to be,
And leave no vestige of a former grace.
But if thy spirit seeks a longer day,
Beyond the reach of winter’s heavy wing,
Then cast thy shadow in a newer way,
To wake the echoes of a second spring.
For beauty kept is but a candle’s end,
But beauty shared is light that hath no end.

II

The silent clock upon the ivory wall,
Doth mark the theft of every passing breath,
And though the cedar stands both proud and tall,
It waits the slow and certain march of death.
I see the gold of summer turn to grey,
And velvet leaves are scattered to the gale,
While all the glories of the bright display,
Behind the mist of time begin to fail.
Yet in the temple of my inward thought,
Thy constant image holds a sacred throne;
A miracle by nature’s hand inwrought,
In colors that the earth hath never known.
Though years may strip the forest of its green,
Within my mind thy summer still is seen.

III

Look on the mirror of the quiet lake,
Wherein the willow views its drooping form,
And see how ripples on the surface break,
Before the coming of the sudden storm.
Why dost thou hoard the riches of thy soul,
To perish in a cold and lonely cell?
The parts of life must seek a greater whole,
Or bide in silence where the shadows dwell.
The sun gives heat to every thirsty grain,
And clouds return their bounty to the deep;
But thou dost seek to make all giving vain,
And in a barren vault thy treasures keep.
O wake the seed before the season dies,
Lest in thy breast the only harvest lies.

IV

The lute is hushed within the marble hall,
The singer’s voice hath lost its ancient fire,
And shadows on the dusty curtain fall,
To damp the embers of a long desire.
Why shouldst thou walk the lonely path alone,
With none to catch the music of thy heart?
A single note is but a hollow moan,
That from the concord lives too far apart.
See how the vines around the trellis twine,
To find a strength they could not hold above;
Their mingled branches in a knot combine,
The living emblem of a nesting love.
The song is lost that hath no echoing ear,
But doubled lives when two are drawn to hear.

V

The sculptor carves the stone with patient hand,
To catch the lightning of a fleeting glance,
But shifting tides shall wash away the sand,
And break the circles of the weary dance.
The tyrant Time, with iron-pointed pen,
Doth cross the lines of beauty from the brow,
And makes a desert of the haunts of men,
Where once the laurel and the rose did bow.
Yet there remains a craft beyond the knife,
To carve a likeness in a softer clay;
To breathe the spirit of a fading life,
Into the morning of a younger day.
Then let thy form in other eyes be born,
To mock the night and meet the rising morn.

VI

I seek no pearls from out the southern main,
To deck the simple measure of my verse,
Nor do I borrow from a foreign strain,
A borrowed glory to my soul’s reverse.
My words are but the servants of thy light,
And find their purpose in thy quiet praise;
They seek no stars to make the darkness bright,
Save those that in thy steady vision blaze.
Let poets boast of myths and legends old,
And clothe their fancies in a cloth of gold;
My story is in plainer habit told,
A tale of love that never shall grow cold.
The truth of thee is art enough for me,
To set the spirit of my longing free.

VII

When I am vanished like a morning mist,
And all my labors are to dust returned,
When lips that once the cup of sorrow kissed,
Have felt the peace for which the weary yearned;
This humble page shall stand against the gale,
A lonely watchman in the dark of time,
To tell the future of a wondrous tale,
Preserved within the amber of a rhyme.
Though brass shall melt and granite wear away,
And kingdoms fall beneath the heavy tread,
The light of thee shall never know decay,
Nor join the ranks of the forgotten dead.
As long as eyes can trace the written sign,
Thy living grace shall through these letters shine.

VIII

My soul is like a vessel on the deep,
That seeks the harbor of thy quiet breast;
While all the winds of heaven are asleep,
It finds in thee its only place of rest.
I bring no tribute but a faithful mind,
No wealth but what thy presence hath bestowed;
In every look a hidden world I find,
To cheer the burden of the weary road.
Thou art the pilot of my drifting thought,
The steady lamp that burns across the wave;
By thy sweet influence is my spirit taught,
To be the master and no more the slave.
Though storms may rise and bitter tempests blow,
Toward thy shore my constant heart shall go.

IX

If in the years to come some hand should find,
These broken relics of a heart’s desire,
And wonder at the passion of a mind,
That burned with such a fierce and holy fire;
Let them not judge the weakness of the phrase,
Or mock the faltering of a mortal tongue,
But look beyond the curtain of the days,
To see the vision that the poet sung.
For though the singer be but common clay,
The theme he touched was of a heavenly birth;
A spark of light that cannot pass away,
To join the ashes of the weary earth.
Then cherish not the vessel, but the wine,
For all that lives within these lines is thine.

X

When darkness gathers round my lonely head,
And hope is like a candle in the wind,
When all the joys of former days are fled,
And left a bitter emptiness behind;
I turn my gaze toward the inner sky,
Wherein thy memory like a planet glows,
To still the tremor of a heavy sigh,
And find the quiet that the spirit knows.
The world may offer honors and renown,
To those who struggle in the dusty race,
But I would cast away a golden crown,
To find the mercy of thy gentle face.
For in thy love a richer kingdom lies,
Than all the glory under mortal skies.

XI

The learned scholar reads the ancient scroll,
To find the secrets of the hidden earth,
And seeks to measure with a narrow soul,
The heavy mystery of a human birth.
But I have found a wisdom more profound,
Within the silence of a single look;
A deeper knowledge than was ever bound,
Between the covers of a dusty book.
Thy presence is the school where I am taught,
The grammar of the heart and of the eye;
By thy sweet logic is my spirit caught,
To learn the truths that never fade or die.
Let others seek the stars to find their fate,
I find my heaven at thy quiet gate.

XII

The sun hath finished his appointed toil,
And sinks to rest within the purple west;
The weary plowman quits the heavy soil,
To find the comfort of a quiet rest.
But in my mind a restless spirit wakes,
To start a journey through the fields of night;
The path of sleep my eager soul forsakes,
To seek the vision of thy distant light.
Across the miles of shadow and of stone,
My thoughts like birds on silent pinions fly;
They cannot bear to leave me here alone,
Beneath the arches of a hollow sky.
So though the body in the darkness lies,
My spirit finds its home within thine eyes.

XIII

The world is full of voices and of song,
Of empty praises and of hollow speech,
Wherein the weak are trampled by the strong,
And nothing stays within the mortal reach.
But thy sweet name is like a quiet bell,
That calls the wanderer from the stormy way;
A secret charm that works a holy spell,
To turn the darkness into sudden day.
Thou art the source of every noble thought,
The quiet center of a world of change;
By thy perfection is my spirit taught,
To find a beauty that is new and strange.
Then let the world its noisy path pursue,
While I remain to love and honor you.

XIV

How can I speak of thee as separate,
When all my life is woven with thy own?
The cruel hand of all-devouring fate,
Can never break the seeds that we have sown.
For as the river mingles with the sea,
And loses name within the vast embrace,
So is my spirit swallowed up in thee,
To find its meaning in thy quiet grace.
Then let the miles between us stretch and grow,
And let the seasons work their weary will;
The more I wander and the further go,
The more I find thy presence with me still.
For though our bodies may be far apart,
Thou art the heartbeat of my inner heart.

XV

I give thee all the love that I can give,
A humble offering to a sacred shrine;
For in thy breath my spirit learns to live,
And all my better thoughts are truly thine.
Then do not chide me for the words I say,
Or think my passion is a passing dream;
It is the sun that lights my weary day,
The steady current of a living stream.
And if I falter in the path I tread,
Or lose the vision of the goal I seek,
Let thy sweet influence on my soul be shed,
To give a courage to the faint and weak.
For thou art all the strength that I possess,
My only comfort in the wilderness.

XVI

My heart and eye have made a secret pact,
To share the glory of thy quiet face;
The one to keep the memory intact,
The other to behold thy living grace.
When thou art absent from my weary sight,
My heart unfolds the picture it hath kept;
To make a morning of the heavy night,
While all the senses of the world have slept.
But when thy presence fills the quiet room,
My eye takes up the banquet of the day;
To chase away the shadows and the gloom,
And drive the phantoms of the mind away.
Thus do they work in harmony and peace,
To give my longing soul a sweet release.

XVII

The seasons change and winter follows fast,
To strip the blossom from the shaking bough;
The joys of summer are too short to last,
And leave but furrows on the weary brow.
But in the garden of my constant mind,
Thy beauty blooms in an eternal spring;
A sacred shelter from the bitter wind,
Wherein the birds of hope forever sing.
No frost can touch the petals of thy grace,
No storm can break the branches of thy love;
It is a quiet and a holy place,
Beyond the clouds that gather far above.
Though all the world should turn to ice and snow,
Within my heart thy roses still shall grow.

XVIII

The world is full of shadows and of lies,
Of broken promises and hearts of stone;
Wherein the spirit of the noble dies,
And leaves the seeker wandering alone.
But thou art like a beacon in the dark,
A steady flame that never shall expire;
The quiet haven for my weary bark,
The holy altar of a pure desire.
I find in thee a truth that cannot fail,
A kindness that no malice can remove;
A shield of silver and a coat of mail,
To guard the treasure of a faithful love.
Then let the world its bitter course pursue,
While I find all my hope and joy in you.

XIX

The stars may fall from out the midnight sky,
And mountains crumble to the level plain;
The very sun within the heavens die,
And all the works of mortal men be vain.
But love shall stand against the wreck of time,
A solid tower on a shifting sand;
A sacred music and a holy rhyme,
That none but faithful hearts can understand.
It is the anchor in the stormy bay,
The quiet center of the turning wheel;
A light that leads us on the weary way,
And gives a comfort that the soul can feel.
Though all things else should perish and depart,
Love is the kingdom of the human heart.

XX

The day is done and all the fires are low,
The shadows lengthen on the quiet floor;
The weary travelers to their rest must go,
And silence gathers at the open door.
I lay my pen aside and close the book,
To find the peace that only love can bring;
To give the world a last and lingering look,
Before I fold my spirit’s tired wing.
I have no fear of what the night may hold,
Or what the coming of the dawn may be;
For in my heart a treasure I enfold,
That sets the spirit of my longing free.
Thus ends the song, but not the love I bear,
Which lives forever in the quiet air.

XXI
When I behold the stars that light the sky,
And see how brief their silver glow remains,
I think on thee and how thou shalt not die,
Though time doth bind the world in heavy chains.
Thy beauty like a morning rose doth bloom,
Which frost and winter seek to steal away,
But my poor pen shall build for thee a room,
Where thou shalt live beyond the reach of day.
Let others seek the gold of earthly kings,
Or boast of titles that shall fade to dust,
I find my wealth in what my spirit sings,
And in thy heart place all my holy trust.
    For though the sun must set and shadows fall,
    Thy name shall live when time hath taken all.

XXII
A silent garden in the winter's sleep,
Where ev'ry leaf is lost to bitter cold,
Is like the world that doth its beauty keep,
Until thy presence makes the barren gold.
The sullen earth forgets the bloom of spring,
And clouds do hide the glory of the sun,
But to thy grace my weary soul doth cling,
Until the race of mortal days be run.
Though rugged storms may batter down the wall,
And leave the fields to wither and decay,
Thy love shall stand and never fear to fall,
But shine as bright as any summer's day.
    So let the seasons change and winter bite,
    Thy face shall be my only source of light.

XXIII
The words of men are but a feeble sound,
That pass away like mist upon the hill,
For in thy face a better truth is found,
That bids the noise of all the world be still.
My tongue may fail to tell the love I bear,
And silence take the place of holy vows,
But in my heart thy image is so fair,
That heaven's self the secret truth allows.
Look not for praise in what my lips may speak,
For eloquence is lost in thy regard,
The strongest voice is render'd faint and weak,
And findeth all its honest courage marr'd.
    Then read the silent language of mine eye,
    Which speaks the truth that speech must ever lie.

XXIV
My heart is like a vessel on the sea,
That bears the treasure of thy love afar,
No stormy wave shall take my soul from thee,
Nor hide the light of thy most blessed star.
Though winds may rage and heavy billows roll,
To sink the hopes of ev'ry sailor's breast,
Thou art the only anchor of my soul,
The only port where I may find my rest.
Let others seek the gems of India's shore,
Or dive for pearls beneath the silent deep,
I possess thee and need for nothing more,
But in my heart thy precious favour keep.
    For what are all the riches of the main,
    If thy sweet love be lost to me again?

XXV
The sun doth climb the highest hill of noon,
To gild the world with golden majesty,
But weary age shall overtake him soon,
And hide his light beneath the darkened sea.
Thy youth is now the glory of the day,
And beauty's lamp doth burn with steady flame,
But time shall steal thy ornaments away,
And leave thee but a shadow of thy name.
Then let thy virtue be the light that stays,
When outward show is lost to silent dust,
To guide thee through the winter of thy days,
And keep the covenant of holy trust.
    For though the flesh may wither and decline,
    The spirit's glory shall for-ever shine.

XXVI
Mine eye and heart are lock'd in deadly strife,
To see who shall possess thy image most,
The eye doth claim the vision of thy life,
The heart doth of thy hidden virtue boast.
The eye would feast upon thy outward grace,
And trace the lines of ev'ry godly part,
But finds no room for any other face,
Within the secret chambers of the heart.
The heart denies the eye its lawful right,
And says that beauty is a thing unseen,
That dwells beyond the reach of mortal sight,
Where only faith and constancy have been.
    A jury of my thoughts shall judge the cause,
    And find that both are bound by love's own laws.

XXVII
Though miles divide us and the way be long,
My thoughts do make a pilgrimage to thee,
And in my heart I hear a silent song,
That speaks of love and fondest memory.
The weary world may keep us far apart,
And substance bind me to this sullen ground,
But thou art ever present in my heart,
Where never-ending joy is to be found.
I see thy face in ev'ry passing cloud,
And hear thy voice in ev'ry gentle breeze,
Though sorrow wraps me in a leaden shroud,
Thy shadow brings my troubled spirit ease.
    For space is but a shadow of the mind,
    When hearts in holy union are entwined.

XXVIII
The tide doth ebb and flow upon the sand,
To leave the shore both barren and defiled,
So beauty drifts away from ev'ry hand,
By time's unfeeling cruelty beguiled.
The rose of youth must lose its crimson hue,
And yield its scent to winter's icy breath,
For ev'ry fair is lost to what is new,
And finds a closet in the arms of death.
But in the lines of this my humble verse,
Thy grace shall find a stay against the grave,
Where future ages shall thy praise rehearse,
And from the reach of dark oblivion save.
    So long as men shall read what I have writ,
    Thy matchless beauty shall not fade a whit.

XXIX
When I survey the faults that mar my soul,
And see how poor a thing I am to thee,
I wonder how thy love can keep me whole,
And grant such mercy to a wretch like me.
I have no worth to purchase thy regard,
No mighty deeds to boast of in thy sight,
My life is but a field of thistles marr'd,
That finds no favour in the morning light.
But then I think upon thy gentle heart,
Which looks beyond the blotted lines of sin,
To find the better and the purer part,
That dwells the secret spirit deep within.
    And then my sorrow turns to holy joy,
    Which neither time nor fortune can destroy.

XXX
In dreams of night I see thy shadow plain,
And hold thee in a fond and sweet embrace,
But wake to find that all my hope is vain,
And find no vestige of thy lovely face.
The day is dark without thy blessed light,
And ev'ry hour is burdened with regret,
I long for all the visions of the night,
Before the sun of mortal life be set.
For shadows are more real than all I see,
When thou art absent from my aching view,
The world is but a ghostly mockery,
Unless it be restored by thee anew.
    Then let me sleep and dream for-ever-more,
    Of that sweet shore where we have met before.

XXXI
Thy heart is like a book of sacred truth,
Where ev'ry leaf is filled with holy grace,
The record of a fair and spotless youth,
That time himself shall never-more erase.
I read the lines of virtue in thine eyes,
And find a wisdom older than the stars,
A treasure that the worldly man denies,
And keeps behind a thousand iron bars.
Let fools go seek for knowledge in the dust,
Or waste their lives in study of the sky,
I place in thy sweet self my only trust,
And find the truth that shall not fade or die.
    For books may perish and the world decay,
    But thy sweet virtue shall not pass away.

XXXII
The summer fades into the golden fall,
And leaves the woods to mourn the passing heat,
Until the winter spreads a rotten pall,
To wrap the world in sorrow's winding sheet.
So doth the spring of life forsake the brow,
And silver o'er the locks of youthful hair,
The furrows of the field are deeper now,
And time hath taken what was once so fair.
But do not grieve for what is lost to time,
Nor weep for beauty's brief and fleeting day,
For virtue stays within its purest prime,
Though ev'ry outward show must pass away.
    Thy inward worth shall flourish in the cold,
    And keep thy spirit young when thou art old.

XXXIII
Look in thy glass and see the wretched theft,
That time hath made upon thy lovely face,
Of all thy youth there is but little left,
And age hath taken ev'ry former grace.
The wrinkles on thy brow are silent foes,
That speak of years that never shall return,
The dying embers of a crimson rose,
That in the fire of mortal life did burn.
Then turn away from that deceitful view,
And find a better mirror in my heart,
Where thou art ever fair and ever new,
And time himself shall have no lawful part.
    For in my love thy image shall not fade,
    Nor lose its glory to the dreary shade.

XXXIV
Thy single look hath power to restore,
The broken spirit and the weary mind,
Or strike a wound that festers ever-more,
And leaves the soul both desolate and blind.
One frown of thine is like a bitter frost,
That kills the bud of hope before it bloom,
Whereby the glory of the world is lost,
And hidden in a deep and silent tomb.
But when thou smilest on my humble state,
The heavens open with a blessed light,
I fear no more the cruel hand of fate,
Nor all the shadows of the dreary night.
    Then be thou kind and grant a gentle ray,
    To turn my darkness into summer's day.

XXXV
I hold thy love upon a lawful lease,
And pay the rent in sighs and bitter tears,
But fear the day when all our joy must cease,
And find its end within the passing years.
The bond is signed with blood and holy vows,
To bind our hearts in union ever-more,
Yet time a quick determinance allows,
And seeks to drive us from the blessed shore.
I owe a debt that I can never pay,
For all the grace that thou hast shown to me,
And though I give my spirit ev'ry day,
I am a bankrupt in my love for thee.
    Then let me be thy captive and thy slave,
    And find my freedom only in the grave.

XXXVI
Thy voice is like the music of the spheres,
That speaks to heaven in a silent strain,
To charm away the sorrow of the years,
And bring a balm to ev'ry mortal pain.
The harps of men are but a jarring noise,
Compared to what thy gentle breath doth give,
The sum of all our earthly fleeting joys,
The only reason why I seek to live.
Let singers boast of all their subtle art,
And tune their strings to catch the passing ear,
I find my music only in thy heart,
Where ev'ry note is holy and is dear.
    For when thou speakest all the world is still,
    To hear the passage of thy godly will.

XXXVII
The world is full of outward show and pride,
That masks the empty spirit deep untold,
Whereby a thousand filthy blots do hide,
Beneath a garment of a glitt'ring gold.
But thou art true in substance and in thought,
And wearest no deceit upon thy brow,
A godly work that nature's self hath wrought,
To whom the spirits of the world must bow.
Thy face is but the herald of thy soul,
Where ev'ry virtue hath its lawful seat,
To keep the purity of nature whole,
And make the sum of perfect grace complete.
    Let others paint their beauty on the skin,
    Thy glory dwells the sacred heart within.

XXXVIII
The stars that shine within the vault of night,
Have no dominion o'er my mortal fate,
For in thy eyes I find a better light,
That guides me through the narrow earthly gate.
They say the planets rule the lives of men,
And point the way to glory or despair,
But I shall find no wisdom in my pen,
Unless I find thy sacred image there.
Thou art my fortune and my holy star,
The only sun that rules my daily sky,
To lead my weary spirit from afar,
And grant the hope that shall not fade or die.
    Then let the heavens turn and seasons flee,
    My only destiny is found in thee.

XXXIX
We are but one, though we be twain in name,
Two hearts that beat within a single breast,
By one desire and one sweet spirit's flame,
To find within our love a perfect rest.
How can I praise the better part of me,
And not be guilty of a vain self-love?
For ev'ry virtue that I find in thee,
Is born of heaven in the world above.
Then let us live as though we were apart,
That I may give to thee the honour due,
And keep the secret chambers of my heart,
For-ever spotless and for-ever true.
    For in our union we are made so whole,
    That death himself shall not divide the soul.

XL
O weary Time, do what thou wilt to me,
And break the mighty oak with heavy age,
Thou shalt not touch the grace that I can see,
Nor tear it from the world in cruel rage.
Thou shalt not touch the glory of my love,
Nor steal the treasure of the blessed mind,
Which dwells within the sacred halls above,
And leaves the dregs of mortal dust behind.
My verse shall be a rampart and a wall,
To stay thy scythe and keep his image fair,
Until the stars and heavens start to fall,
And silence takes the burden of the air.
    For though thou kill the flesh and strike the bone,
    His matchless grace shall ever-more be known.
