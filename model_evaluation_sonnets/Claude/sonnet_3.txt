III

Look inward, soul, and tell me what thou seest:
A house whose rooms were furnished long ago,
Where memory and regret conspire to feast
On what was dear and what was let to go.
Yet somewhere in that crumbling, candled hall
There hangs a portrait bright against the stone,
A face unmarred by time, unmoved by fall,
More vivid than the things I call my own.
What painter set such colour to the eye,
What art contrived that smile so full of grace,
That neither memory's damp nor sorrow's sigh
Can dim the lustre shining from that face?
  It is no painter's hand that set thee there,
  But love itself, which keeps all true things fair.
