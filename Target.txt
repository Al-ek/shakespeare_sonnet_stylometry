XXI
If I have loved thee badly, blame the tongue,
Which is a clumsy servant to the soul;
The feeling far outreaches what is sung,
As rivers always wider than their shoal.
For what I mean is vaster than my means,
A country that my language cannot map,
Whose mountains rise behind my written scenes
And dwarf the little lines I set on paper's lap.
Yet what is love but this: to feel the gap
Between the word and what the word would say,
And cross it still, and fall, and close the gap
Again tomorrow as I failed today.
To love is to attempt the uncrossable:
I cross it still, though crossing is impossible.
