XLI

When gold-wing'd dawn doth wake the sleeping rose,
And bids the silver dew to melt in air,
So doth thy beauty to the world disclose
A grace more rich than any flower can bear.
But as the sun must haste to meet the night,
And leave the garden to the winter’s frost,
So time shall steal the luster of thy light,
And all thy youthful pride in age be lost.
Yet let this verse a living mirror be,
Wherein thy summer may for ever dwell;
Though nature fades, the world in these shall see
The sovereign worth that I have loved so well.
    Then let the scythe of time do what it must,
    Thy fame shall live when thou art turn’d to dust.
