XLVII

When forty winters more have spent their rage,
And snows of time do crown thy golden head,
How will it fare with thee in that old age,
When all the fires of youthful lust are dead?
Then shall thy mirror show a wrinkled brow,
Where now the smooth and lily skin doth lie;
And those who gaze upon thy beauty now,
Will turn their looks and pass thee coldly by.
But in these lines thy spring shall never end,
For verse hath power to stay the hand of fate;
To every age thy beauty I will lend,
And keep thee ever in thy prime of state.
    So long as men have eyes to read my song,
    Thou shalt be fair, and I in love be strong.
