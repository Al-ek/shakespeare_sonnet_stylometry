II

Behold the oak that braved a hundred years,
Now stripped of leaves by autumn’s heavy hand,
Its mighty boughs, once dry of winter’s tears,
Now cast a lonely shadow o’er the land.
So shall thy strength, which now doth seem so vast,
Be humbled by the creeping pace of age,
Until thy glory is a thing of past,
A faded line upon a dusty page.
Then where is hid the fire that warmed thy blood,
When all thy pulses beat with heavy lead?
The ebbing tide shall swallow up the flood,
And leave the river’s weary channel dead.
But in a child, thy spirit finds its way,
To hold the night and bring another day.
