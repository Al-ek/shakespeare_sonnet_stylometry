IV

Why dost thou spend thy beauty on the air,
And give thy riches to the passing wind?
For nature lent thee all that is so fair,
To be returned, not selfishly confined.
The usurer of grace must pay the debt,
When heavy years demand their final toll,
And leave thee with a burden of regret,
Within the silent chambers of thy soul.
But if thy likeness walketh on the earth,
When thou art gone into the silent deep,
The world shall find a second, better birth,
And wake the wonder that the shadows keep.
Then be not cold to fortune’s kind decree,
But leave a map of what we find in thee.
