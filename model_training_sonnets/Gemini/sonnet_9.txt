IX

The miser hoards his gold in iron cells,
And counts his coins with fingers thin and cold,
But in his heart a lonely hunger dwells,
For things that never can be bought or sold.
Thy beauty is a treasure far more deep,
Than all the riches of the eastern sea,
A gift that thou shouldst never strive to keep,
But spend with love and wild economy.
For gold will tarnish in the damp and dark,
And wealth will vanish like a morning mist,
Leaving no trace, no shadow, and no mark,
Of all the pleasures that the senses kissed.
But love, once given, grows a thousand fold,
A wealth of spirit that can ne'er grow old.
