XII

When I do count the ringing of the clock
And see the silver morning turn to grey,
And watch the violet wilt upon the stock
And the dark curls of youth go pale away;
When autumn's furrows hold the summer dead
And summer's harvest sweated on the cart,
I see that beauty hath a price to dread,
Which time doth take, and nothing else apart.
And nothing stands against the blade of years,
Not stone, not steel, not beauty's fairest face,
Save only offspring grown from happy tears,
Who carry beauty on in time and space.
  Against time's scythe there is no other shield
  But children born from beauty's gracious yield.
