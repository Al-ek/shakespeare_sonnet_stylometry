XXIX

Some loves are tempests, vast and violent things,
That scatter sense and set the judgment reeling,
That lift the lover up on stormward wings
And dash him down without regard for feeling.
But ours hath been the steady river's kind,
That carves the stone by constancy not rage,
That seeks its course with clear and patient mind,
And does not rush, but deepens age by age.
I would not have the lightning for a hearth,
Nor hurricanes to serve as lullabies,
Give me the love that knows its settled worth
And meets me ever under open skies.
  Let tempests thrill the hearts of braver men—
  I choose the calm that draws thee back again.
