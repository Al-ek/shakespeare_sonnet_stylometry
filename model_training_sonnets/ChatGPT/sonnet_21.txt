XXI

When first I marked the summer of thy face,
It shamed the rose and made the lily pale;
For in thy glance did sovereign beauty place
A court where all proud ornaments must fail.
Thy voice, more sweet than lark at break of day,
Commands the winds to hush their roaming flight;
And time itself, that steals all things away,
Stands still, arrested by thy living light.
Yet fear I this: that hours, with envious hand,
Will crop the bloom that now so rich appears,
And write their ruin where thy graces stand,
As frost doth blight the pride of vernal years.
O, lend thy form to verse that cannot die,
So shalt thou live when all thy charms deny.
