III

If I compare thee to the budding spring,
Thou art more gentle, yet more full of fire;
No fickle breeze such constancy can bring,
Nor blooms so rich as those thou dost inspire.
For spring must yield to summer’s ardent reign,
And summer fade before the autumn’s breath,
But thou remain’st untouched by time or pain,
A triumph wrested from the grasp of death.
What secret grace preserves thee ever fair?
What hidden power defies the world’s decline?
No season dares to lay its changes there,
For all their beauty seems but theft from thine.
Thus Nature bows and yields her borrowed art,
Confessing thee her masterwork of heart.
