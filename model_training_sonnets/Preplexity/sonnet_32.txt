XXXII

My pen grows faint to trace thy noble praise,
Its ink unworthy of so clean a thought;
Yet still it strives, as lover’s duty sways,
To carve what even angels leave untaught.
For virtue’s form in thee so purely dwells,
It shames the frailties flesh must still confess;
And though my weak expression fails and quells,
The effort owns its failure not the less.
If all my words should perish unrehearsed,
And wind erase what heart in fire devise,
Then still thy worth in silence stands reversed—
Thy very being speaks beyond the wise.
I write, for silence too would blush to hide,
That such perfection ever lived and died.
